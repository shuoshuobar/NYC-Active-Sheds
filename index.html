City Council Cracks Down on Sidewalk Sheds

Yushuo Wang — May 4, 2025

An unintended street view can be interrupted by those green metal structures. Sidewalk sheds that clutter New York City have become an almost permanent part of the landscape. This week, the Department of Buildings reported 8,547 active sheds, as the City Council passed new rules limiting how long sheds remain standing to reclaim public space.

In late March, the Council passed new rules to limit how long sidewalk sheds can stay up. The standard shed permit is now cut from one year to three months, except in cases involving building demolition, alterations or new construction.

Property owners must now submit proof of ongoing work to renew a permit. Failing to comply entails fines of up to $6,000 a month, or $200 per linear foot.

“These are all really important design changes for the city of New York. We will meet our safety needs while also looking like every other city that does not have a skyline filled with sheds and scaffolding,” the legislation’ leading sponsor, Manhattan Councilman Keith Powers, said at a press conference before the vote.

The green, hulking structures are meant to protect pedestrians from falling debris. However, more than 300 remained in place for over five years.

The city’s current oldest shed — still standing on Second Avenue in Manhattan — has been in place for 15 years. Another long-standing structure sat in front of the Office of the Chief Medical Examiner on First Avenue for nearly the same time. It was removed last August after city contractors completed facade repairs.

Also included in the most recent legislative package, sheds must now be at least 12 feet tall, up from the previous eight. Color options can range from white to metallic gray, or match the building's façade, moving beyond the standard hunter green.

Mayor Eric Adams signed the shed reform bills into law on April 17, along with his “Get Sheds Down” initiative in 2023. The Department of Buildings is to conduct a study and recommend updated sidewalk shed designs by the end of September this year.

“They will allow us to reclaim valuable space for the public and let the light back onto our sidewalk,” the mayor said.
